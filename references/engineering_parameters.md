# The 39 Engineering Parameters

These are Altshuller's generic system characteristics, abstracted from the study of hundreds of thousands of patents. Use them to translate a specific, concrete problem ("our packaging crushes in shipping") into a generic technical contradiction ("Strength vs. Weight of moving object") that the Contradiction Matrix can work with.

Translation is the hard part of using the matrix well. The parameters are deliberately generic — "Force," "Shape," "Loss of information" — so a real engineering detail almost never maps onto exactly one of them. Expect to round, generalize, and sometimes formulate the same contradiction two or three different ways before the matrix returns something useful.

An **object** below can mean the whole technical system, a subsystem, or a single part — whichever level the contradiction is being analyzed at. **Moving** objects can change position in space (on their own or under external force); **stationary/binding** objects do not (e.g., a building, a foundation, a housing).

## Cluster 1 — Common physical and geometric parameters

1. **Weight of moving object** — mass of a moving object in a gravitational field; the force it exerts on its support or suspension.
2. **Weight of stationary object** — same, for an object that does not change position.
3. **Length of moving object** — a linear geometric dimension of a moving object.
4. **Length of stationary object** — same, for a stationary object.
5. **Area of moving object** — the surface a moving object occupies or covers.
6. **Area of stationary object** — same, for a stationary object.
7. **Volume of moving object** — the space (internal or external) a moving object occupies.
8. **Volume of stationary object** — same, for a stationary object.
9. **Speed** — velocity; rate of a process or action in time.
10. **Force** — any interaction capable of changing an object's condition.
11. **Tension, pressure** — stress on or inside the object.
12. **Shape** — the external contours/boundaries that separate the object from its environment or other objects.
13. **Stability of object** — the object's ability to maintain its integrity over time; resistance to wear, decomposition, disassembly.
14. **Strength** — the object's ability to resist a change of state in response to force; resistance to breaking.
17. **Temperature** — thermal condition of the object, including related thermal parameters like heat capacity.
18. **Brightness, illumination** — light flux per unit area; other illumination characteristics.
21. **Power** — the rate of energy usage by which the object's functions are performed.

(See also parameters 13–16 and 26, which straddle clusters.)

## Cluster 2 — Technique-independent negative parameters

15. **Durability of moving object** — duration over which a moving object performs useful/neutral functions (service life, mean time between failures).
16. **Durability of stationary object** — same, for a stationary object.
19. **Energy spent by moving object** — energy required by a moving object to perform its function.
20. **Energy spent by stationary object** — same, for a stationary object.
22. **Waste of energy** — energy use (e.g. heat) that does not contribute to the job being done. Distinct from 19/20: reducing *waste* often needs different heuristics than reducing *consumption*.
23. **Waste of substance** — partial or complete, permanent or temporary loss of material.
24. **Loss of information** — partial or complete loss of data or access to data, including sensory data.
25. **Waste of time** — time taken out of an activity; "cycle time."
26. **Amount of substance** — the quantity of material or elements that can be changed.
30. **Harmful factors acting on object** — the object's susceptibility to externally generated harmful effects.
31. **Harmful side effects** — a harmful effect generated by the object itself as a byproduct of its normal operation.

(See also parameters 14, 36, 37.)

## Cluster 3 — Technique-independent positive parameters

13. **Stability of object** *(also listed above)*
14. **Strength** *(also listed above)*
27. **Reliability** — the object's ability to perform its intended function predictably.
28. **Accuracy of measurement** — closeness of a measured value to the actual value.
29. **Accuracy of manufacturing** — closeness of actual characteristics to specified/required characteristics, achieved during production.
32. **Manufacturability** — ease/effortlessness of manufacturing or fabricating the object.
33. **Convenience of use** — simplicity and ease of operation; fewer steps, less specialized skill or tooling required.
34. **Repairability** — convenience, simplicity, and speed of repairing faults or defects.
35. **Adaptability** — the object's ability to respond positively to external changes; versatility across circumstances.
36. **Complexity of device** — number and diversity of elements and their interrelationships; how hard the object is to master.
37. **Complexity of control** — how difficult, costly, and time-consuming it is to measure or monitor the object; fuzzy or interfering relationships between components.
38. **Level of automation** — the object's ability to perform its functions without human interface, from manually-operated tool up to fully self-programming and self-monitoring.
39. **Productivity** — number of functions/operations performed per unit time; output per unit time or cost per unit output.

(See also parameters 19–21.)

---

**Note:** the boundaries between these three clusters are not sharp, and the assignment of a given parameter to "positive" or "negative" is somewhat arbitrary — they're a study aid, not a rigid taxonomy.

There have been various proposed extensions to this list over the decades (e.g. Mann et al.'s "Matrix 2003," which expands to 48 parameters). This skill uses Altshuller's original 39, which remain the most widely taught, the most extensively cross-referenced in the literature, and the version the classic Contradiction Matrix is built on.
