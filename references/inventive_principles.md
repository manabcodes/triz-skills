# The 40 Inventive Principles

Generalized solution patterns Altshuller extracted from studying high-level patents. Each one is a *direction to think in*, not a ready-made answer — you still have to translate the abstract principle into something concrete for your actual system. When the Contradiction Matrix returns a set of principle numbers for your contradiction, read the matching entries below and brainstorm against each sub-point (A, B, C...) in turn; don't stop at the first one that sounds promising.

---

**1. Segmentation (Fragmentation)**
A. Divide an object into independent parts. *(Multiwire cables; replacing a mainframe with networked PCs; a cargo ship built from identical interchangeable sections.)*
B. Make an object modular/easy to assemble and disassemble. *(Modular furniture; quick-disconnect plumbing joints.)*
C. Increase the degree of fragmentation. *(Venetian blinds instead of a solid shade; powdered welding metal instead of rod, for better joint penetration.)*

**2. Extraction (Removal)**
A. Separate an interfering part or property from an object, or extract just the part/property you need. *(Move a noisy compressor outside the building; use fiber optics to pipe light away from a hot source.)*

**3. Local quality**
A. Change a uniform structure or environment to a non-uniform one. *(Doping silicon wafers only in specific photolithographic windows.)*
B. Make each part of an object function under the conditions best suited to it. *(A lunch box with separate hot and cold compartments.)*
C. Make each part fulfill a different, useful function. *(Pencil with eraser; multi-tool.)*

**4. Asymmetry**
A. Change an object's shape from symmetrical to asymmetrical. *(Asymmetric mixing vessels improve mixing; one side of a tire reinforced more than the other to resist curb impacts.)*
B. If already asymmetrical, increase the degree of asymmetry.

**5. Merging (Combining)**
A. Merge identical or similar objects, or assemble parts to perform operations in parallel. *(Networked PCs; millions of transistors on one chip.)*
B. Make operations contiguous or parallel in time. *(Slats linked together in venetian blinds; medical instruments that test multiple blood parameters at once.)*

**6. Universality**
A. Make a part or object perform multiple functions, eliminating the need for other parts. *(Toothbrush handle that holds toothpaste; sofa-bed.)*

**7. Nesting (Nested doll)**
A. Place one object inside another, recursively. *(Measuring cups; Russian nesting dolls.)*
B. Make one part pass through a cavity in another. *(Telescoping antenna; retractable landing gear.)*

**8. Counterweight (Anti-weight)**
A. Merge an object with another that provides lift, to counter its weight. *(Foaming agent in a log bundle so it floats better.)*
B. Compensate for weight by interacting with the environment (aerodynamic, hydrodynamic, buoyant forces). *(Aircraft wing shape creates lift via pressure differential.)*

**9. Prior counteraction**
A. If an action has both useful and harmful effects, pre-apply an opposing action to control the harm. *(Buffer a solution in advance to prevent pH-extreme damage.)*
B. Pre-stress an object to oppose undesirable forces it will later experience. *(Pre-stressing rebar before pouring concrete.)*

**10. Prior action**
A. Perform a required change (fully or partially) before it's needed. *(Pre-pasted wallpaper; self-adhesive stamps.)*
B. Pre-arrange objects so they can act from the most convenient position, with no delivery delay. *(A scalpel blade pre-positioned in a surgical cast, ready to cut the cast off later.)*

**11. Beforehand cushioning**
A. Prepare emergency/backup means in advance to compensate for an object's relatively low reliability. *(Backup parachute; tape backup of critical data.)*

**12. Equipotentiality**
A. In a potential field (e.g. gravity), change conditions so objects don't need to be raised or lowered. *(Canal locks; engine oil changed from a pit below the car instead of lifting the car.)*

**13. Inversion (The other way around)**
A. Invert the action used to solve the problem. *(Cool the inner part instead of heating the outer part, to loosen a stuck assembly.)*
B. Make movable parts fixed and fixed parts movable. *(A treadmill: the "ground" moves, the person stays still.)*
C. Turn the object/process upside down. *(Invert a railcar to empty grain instead of scooping it out.)*

**14. Spheroidality (Curvature)**
A. Replace flat/rectilinear parts with curved ones. *(Arches and domes for structural strength.)*
B. Use rollers, balls, spirals, domes. *(Ball-point pens; computer mouse ball.)*
C. Go from linear to rotary motion; use centrifugal force. *(Spin-drying clothes instead of wringing.)*

**15. Dynamism (Dynamics)**
A. Allow an object's characteristics to change to find an optimal operating condition. *(Adjustable steering wheel.)*
B. Divide an object into parts capable of moving relative to each other. *(Scissors instead of a fixed knife.)*
C. If an object is rigid, make it movable/adaptive. *(Flexible endoscope.)*

**16. Partial or excessive action**
A. If 100% of an effect is hard to achieve by a given method, use "slightly more" or "slightly less" of the same method to make the problem easier. *(Overfill then spin off the excess in photolithography; top off a gas tank.)*

**17. Another dimension**
A. Move a 1-D motion problem to 2-D, or a 2-D problem to 3-D. *(A 5-axis cutting tool that reaches into space rather than along one line.)*
B. Use a multi-layer/multi-story arrangement instead of a single layer. *(Multi-CD changer.)*
C. Tilt or reorient the object. *(Dump truck bed.)*
D. Use the other side of a given area. *(Components on both sides of a circuit board.)*

**18. Mechanical vibration**
A. Oscillate or vibrate an object. *(Vibrating a casting mold while filling improves structure.)*
B. If already oscillating, increase the frequency.
C. Use an object's resonant frequency. *(Ultrasonic resonance to break up kidney stones.)*
D. Use piezoelectric vibrators instead of mechanical ones.

**19. Periodic action**
A. Replace a continuous action with a periodic/pulsed one. *(Pulsed warning lamp is more noticeable than one continuously lit.)*
B. If already periodic, change the frequency or magnitude.

**20. Continuity of useful action**
A. Keep all parts of an object working at full load, all the time. *(A flywheel stores energy when a vehicle stops, so the engine keeps running at optimum power.)*
B. Eliminate idle or intermittent action. *(A printer that prints during carriage return as well as the forward stroke.)*

**21. Skipping (Rushing through)**
A. Conduct a harmful, hazardous, or destructive stage of a process at high speed. *(A high-speed dental drill avoids heating tissue.)*

**22. Convert harm into benefit**
A. Use a harmful factor (especially from the environment) to produce a positive effect. *(Use waste heat to generate electricity.)*
B. Eliminate a primary harmful action by combining it with another harmful action that cancels it.
C. Amplify a harmful factor until it's no longer harmful. *(Deliberately setting a backfire to deny a forest fire its fuel.)* Also known as "blessing in disguise."

**23. Feedback**
A. Introduce feedback to improve a process. *(Auto volume control; floating-valve liquid level control.)*
B. If feedback already exists, change its magnitude or influence.

**24. Intermediary (Mediator)**
A. Use an intermediary carrier or process. *(A nail set used between hammer and nail.)*
B. Temporarily merge one object with another that can later be easily removed. *(A pot holder.)*

**25. Self-service**
A. Make an object serve itself with auxiliary helpful functions. *(A soda fountain pump powered by the same CO2 that carbonates the drink.)*
B. Make the object service/repair itself. *(Halogen lamp filaments that regenerate during use.)*
C. Use waste resources, energy, or substances. *(Cogeneration: using process heat to generate electricity.)*

**26. Copying**
A. Use a simpler, cheaper copy instead of an unavailable/expensive/fragile original. *(Virtual reality instead of an expensive vacation.)*
B. Replace an object/process with optical copies. *(Surveying from photographs instead of on the ground.)*
C. Move from visible-light copies to infrared/ultraviolet. *(Infrared imaging to detect heat sources.)*

**27. Cheap short-living objects**
A. Replace an expensive durable object with multiple cheap, disposable ones. *(Disposable medical supplies; single-use cameras.)*

**28. Mechanics substitution**
A. Replace a mechanical means with a sensory one (optical, acoustic, taste, smell). *(An acoustic "fence" for pets instead of a physical one.)*
B. Use electric, magnetic, or electromagnetic fields to interact with the object.
C. Move from static to movable fields, from unstructured to structured fields.
D. Use fields with field-activated particles (e.g. ferromagnetic).

**29. Pneumatics and hydraulics**
A. Use gas or liquid parts instead of solid ones. *(Gel-filled shoe inserts; inflatable structures.)*
B. Use Archimedes' principle (buoyancy) to reduce effective weight.
C. Use negative/vacuum pressure.
D. Use foam — combining the properties of liquid and gas with low weight.

**30. Flexible shells and thin films**
A. Use flexible shells/thin films instead of 3-D structures. *(Inflatable winter covers for tennis courts.)*
B. Isolate an object from its environment using a flexible shell or film. *(Bubble-wrap shipping envelopes.)*

**31. Porous materials**
A. Make an object porous, or add porous elements. *(Drilling holes to reduce weight; porous inserts soaked in coolant for short-term cooling.)*
B. If already porous, use the pores to introduce a useful substance or function. *(Storing hydrogen in a palladium sponge's pores.)*

**32. Color changes**
A. Change the color of an object or its environment. *(Safe-lights in a photographic darkroom.)*
B. Change the transparency of an object or its environment.
C. Use colored additives or luminescent traces to observe otherwise-invisible objects/processes.

**33. Homogeneity**
A. Make objects that interact with a given object out of the same material (or a material with matching properties). *(Container made of the same material as its contents, to reduce chemical reaction.)*

**34. Discarding and recovering**
A. Discard (dissolve, evaporate, etc.) a portion of an object once it's served its function. *(Dissolving medicine capsules; rocket boosters that separate after use.)*
B. Conversely, restore consumable parts during operation. *(Self-sharpening lawnmower blades.)*

**35. Parameter changes (Transformation of properties)**
A. Change an object's physical state (gas/liquid/solid). *(Transporting gas as liquid to reduce volume.)*
B. Change concentration or consistency. *(Concentrated liquid soap, more viscous and easier to dose correctly.)*
C. Change the degree of flexibility.
D. Change temperature.
E. Change other characteristics as needed.

**36. Phase transitions**
A. Use phenomena that occur during a phase transition (volume change, absorption/release of heat). *(Heat pumps exploit vaporization/condensation cycles.)*

**37. Thermal expansion**
A. Use thermal expansion or contraction of materials. *(Cool the inner part / heat the outer part to assemble a tight-fitting joint, then let it return to equilibrium.)*
B. If already using thermal expansion, combine materials with different expansion coefficients. *(Bimetallic strip thermostats.)*

**38. Strong oxidants**
A. Replace normal air with oxygen-enriched air.
B. Replace enriched air with pure oxygen. *(Oxy-acetylene cutting torch.)*
C. Expose air/oxygen to ionizing radiation.
D. Use ionized oxygen.
E. Replace ionized oxygen with ozone.

**39. Inert atmosphere**
A. Replace a normal environment with an inert one. *(Argon atmosphere to prevent oxidation of a hot filament.)*
B. Add neutral parts or inert additives to an object. *(Bulking out powdered detergent with inert filler for easier measuring.)*

**40. Composite materials**
A. Change from a uniform material to a composite (multi-material) one. *(Carbon-fiber composite aircraft wings: high strength, low weight.)*

---

## A note on extensions

Beyond the canonical 40, later TRIZ researchers (cited in Savransky's *Engineering of Creativity*) have proposed additional sub-principles based on continued patent analysis, including a **multistep principle** (efficiency through sequential use of uniform objects, e.g. multi-cylinder engines), **dissociation-association** (molecular-level splitting/recombining, a sub-principle of #34), **use of pauses** (a sub-principle of #19), **use of epenthetic/insert parts** (temporary manufacturing aids later removed), **impedance matching**, and a **concentration-dispersion principle**. These aren't part of the standard Contradiction Matrix lookup but are worth knowing if the 40 standard principles don't yield a workable direction.
